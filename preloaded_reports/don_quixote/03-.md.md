Based on the conversation history, Sancho stayed with Don Quixote after the windmill disaster for a few key reasons:

**Loyalty and trust**: Your highlights show Sancho "believe[s] everything your grace says" and defers to his master despite the obvious madness. He's committed to the role of squire.

**The promise of reward**: Sancho dreams of gaining an ínsula (island governorship) that Quixote has promised him. Even when he jokes about renouncing it, he's clearly motivated by the possibility of advancement beyond his peasant life.

**Practicality over confrontation**: Sancho is pragmatic - he points out the windmills are windmills, tends Quixote's wounds, but then "humors his enchantment excuse rather than argue." It's easier to go along than to fight reality with someone so deep in delusion.

**Simple faith**: There's a sincere credulity in Sancho. He may not fully believe in giants and enchanters, but he believes in *serving* his master and seeing where this leads. The adventure itself, however absurd, offers more than his old life as a farmer.

In essence, Sancho is invested - financially, emotionally, and by the bonds of service. One windmill crash isn't enough to break those ties when dreams of islands and the duties of loyalty keep him riding alongside his mad knight.
