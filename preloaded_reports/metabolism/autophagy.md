## Autophagy & Fasting: The Two Main Types

**1. Macroautophagy (the main type)**
- Cells form double-membrane structures (autophagosomes) that engulf damaged proteins, organelles, and cellular waste
- These fuse with lysosomes and get broken down
- Components are recycled for energy or building new structures

**2. Microautophagy / Lipophagy**
- Direct engulfment of cellular material (including lipid droplets) by lysosomes
- **Lipophagy specifically** = autophagy targeting fat stored within cells
- During fasting, lipid droplets can be degraded in hepatocytes by a specific autophagy-related process called "lipophagy," digesting this storage organelle

## What Happens During Extended Fasting

When you fast for an extended period:

```
Fasting begins
      ↓
Insulin DROPS, Glucagon RISES
      ↓
Fat cells release fatty acids (lipolysis)
      ↓
HIGH circulating fatty acids in blood
      ↓
Liver converts some to ketones
      ↓
Autophagy RAMPS UP
      ↓
Body running on fat + recycled cellular components
```

Acute starvation induces a stereotyped pattern of metabolic alterations, including an increase in blood-borne triglycerides and free fatty acids (FFAs). This is generally accompanied by the secretion of glucagon, as well as a reduction in circulating insulin.

Insulin may regulate diurnal cycles in autophagy flux, which in rat liver peaks at the end of the fasting period and is minimal after refeeding.

## Your Key Question: What Happens When Insulin Returns?

**YES — you're exactly right.**

When you break the fast (especially with carbs):

```
Eat food (especially carbs)
        ↓
Insulin SURGES
        ↓
THREE things happen simultaneously:
        ↓
1. LIPOLYSIS STOPS
   (Fat cells stop releasing fatty acids)
        ↓
2. LIPOGENESIS ACTIVATES
   (Body switches to fat STORAGE mode)
        ↓
3. CIRCULATING FATTY ACIDS GET TAKEN BACK UP
   (Into fat cells and liver)
        ↓
AUTOPHAGY SHUTS DOWN
```

The reintroduction of nutrition to a starved or fasted individual results in a rapid decline in both gluconeogenesis and anaerobic metabolisms. This is mediated by the rapid increase in serum insulin that occurs on refeeding.

During fasting, adipose tissue releases fatty acids into the circulation for uptake by oxidative tissues. After feeding, the flow of fatty acids is reversed and the adipose tissue is replenished.

## The Insulin Signal to Fat Cells

| Fasting State (Low Insulin) | Refeeding State (High Insulin) |
|----------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Fat cells RELEASE fatty acids | Fat cells TAKE UP fatty acids |
| Lipolysis active | Lipolysis suppressed |
| Lipogenesis suppressed | Lipogenesis activated |
| Autophagy HIGH | Autophagy MINIMAL |
| Ketones produced | Ketone production stops |

Lipogenesis is stimulated by a high carbohydrate diet, whereas it is inhibited by polyunsaturated fatty acids and by fasting. Insulin stimulates lipogenesis.

Insulin is the primary hormone which stimulates hepatic lipogenesis in the fed state. Hepatic lipogenesis is low in the fasted state and high in the fed state.

## Why This Matters: Breaking the Fast

If you fast for 24+ hours and have lots of fatty acids circulating, then break your fast with **refined carbs**:

1. Massive insulin spike
2. All those circulating fatty acids? → **Driven back into fat cells**
3. Autophagy immediately suppressed
4. You potentially **undo much of the benefit**

If you break your fast with **protein/fat/low-carb**:

1. Modest insulin response
2. Fatty acids can continue being oxidized
3. Gentler transition out of fasted state
4. Autophagy winds down gradually

---

## The Bottom Line

You've identified a critical insight:

> **Fasting liberates fatty acids into the blood. But if you spike insulin when breaking the fast, those fatty acids get told to go right back into storage.**

This is why many people recommend breaking fasts with:
- Protein (eggs, meat, fish)
- Healthy fats (avocado, nuts)
- Non-starchy vegetables

**NOT** with:
- Bread, pasta, rice
- Sugary foods
- Fruit juice

The goal is to allow a gradual transition where circulating fatty acids continue to be oxidized for energy rather than immediately being shunted back into fat cells by an insulin surge.
